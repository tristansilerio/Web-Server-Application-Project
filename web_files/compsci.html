Computer science is a big place. Here's a woefully incomplete sampling of its many varied and surprising rooms we don't get the chance to visit much at Holy Cross...

Security + Distributed Systems

Besides teaching me how to think and to write, and showing me the meaning of style, Fred Schneider managed to find time to teach me what state machines have to to with security and it means for a distributed system to be secure. languages based security meshes with all of this too, of course. Fred is also good at reminding me of the value to be found in classic work.

Information + Networks

As a grad student, I took Jon Kleinberg's course on the structure of information networks, where I learned a lot about small worlds and what that means for searching, the structure of peer-to-peer networks, and all things graphs. Jon and David Easley have a great book that covers a lot of similar stuff, and the two of them have teamed up with Éva Tardos to create an on-line EdX course on the same topics. If that course is even a fraction as interesting as Jon's live networks course, it is absolutely worth checking out.

Knowledge + Uncertainty + Reasoning + Decisions

Long ago, Joe Halpern was my undergraduate advisor, and I've had the good fortune of hearing him speak on a huge variety of topics since then. Joe tackles some of the really fundamental questions, stuff that underlies not just computer science, but economics, philosophy, economics, the nature of intelligence and, well, pretty much everything: what is knowledge and how do we reason about it; how do we reason in the face of uncertainty; and what is the relationship between knowledge and action.

Big Data + Distributed Systems

Hakim Weatherspoon works on big distributed and networked systems with lots of data. Although I brush the surface of distributed systems a bit in my networks class, and Prof. King recently offered a course on data mining, the systems Hakim deals with are much bigger and use lots of neat techniques for storing data on lots of cheap but uncentralized disk, replicating data to make it reliable, and efficiently retrieving it.

Languages + Security
Andrew Myers showed me how programming languages intersect with security. A lot of his work deals with information flow, how to quantify it, or how we can use it as a way to split a program into pieces. He's got a great blog about security and language too.
